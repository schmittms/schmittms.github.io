Machine learning interpretable models of cell mechanics from protein images

In this work we use deep neural networks to predict traction forces exerted by cells directly from protein microscopy images. We then go on to constrain these networks with an interpretable biomechanical model to reveal how the network uses the information from the protein distributions to make its predictions.

This work was the result of a great collaboration with the Oakes group (Loyola) and the Gardel group (UChicago) and wouldn't have been possible without my co-first author Jonathan Colen.

The paper was published in Cell (2024).

Information theory for data-driven model reduction in physics and biology

In physics, one frequently reduces the description of a complex system to a simpler model that captures its essential features. In the process, one intuitively understands that information is being thrown away. In this work, we make this intuition precise by formulating model reduction as a compression using tools from information theory. Our theoretical results even allow us to understand the latent variables discovered by certain deep neural networks when applied to dynamical systems.

This manuscript is currently under review. A preprint can be found on arXiv.

Data-driven discovery of functional guilds in complex microbial communities

In this ongoing work we tackle the problem of identifying functional groups (or "guilds") of species in complex communities directly from data. These "species" can be literal species of bacteria (for example as measured by 16S sequencing), or they can be genes, proteins, or any other entities that interact in a complex system.

This is work done in collaboration with Kiseok Lee (now U Delaware) and Seppe Kuehn (UChicago). This is currently work in progress.

Geometry adaptation of protrusion and polarity dynamics in confined cell migration

How do cells move through confining spaces? Their motion results from the coordinated interactions of protrusions, adhesions with the environment, and the cell nucleus, and an internal polarity. In this work we combine data-driven inference with a mechanistic bottom-up approach to model the protrusion and polarity dynamics of cells migrating in confining environments. Our model reveals how a feedback loop leads to stereotypical cycles of protrusion-nucleus dynamics that drive the migration of the cell through constrictions.
